The trick pressed another button and the ghosts vanished.

The bandstand at the park seemed as good a place as any to kick it. When she was little scarfing on memorial day hot dogs she’d watch her sister Haley playing the dulcimer with her redneck band here. Streamers popcorn carts some kids would go up and dance.

She sat on the floor against one of the railings and dug into her purse for her spike. She felt the doodad instead and pulled it out. Alternate realities huh.

She turned the dials to fourteen years ago and googled the coordinates to her childhood home on her phone and entered those as well. The surroundings started to shimmy again and the ghosts flicked into view this time dozens of them. Dad’s Superbowl barbeque in the backyard. Her sister Haley had been out of town so she just sat by herself next to the salad table while everyone gathered by the grill and the kegs. She saw Owen Dad’s boss from the auto-repair shop giving her brother a nut tap and lumbering over. He pulled a bill from his padded vest and said “how’d ya like to make a twenty?”

“What for?” her ghost self said looking up. He was wide and tapered up to the top like a dirt mound stacked to six foot three.

“Whaddayasay we go in the tool shed and I show ya what for?”

“Fuck off” she said.

“Don’t be like that now how about fifty?”

“Eat shit I’m fourteen.”

“Stuck-up bitch” he said and left trailing booze stink behind him.

She pressed the off button and the ghosts went away.

She turned the dials to five years after that in Owen’s office. She sees her shriveled nineteen-going-on-forty-five body standing by the door fidgeting as Owen sat at his desk.

“You know yer gonna need to turn a lot more if yer fixin’ to buy a gram a day. Why don’t I switch you to the white stuff?” Owen said.

“I ain’t fuck with no needles” Maggie said.

He turned to her “like you ain’t taken your fair share of pricks hur hur. Come on I got my spike right here I’ll boot ya up.”

“Just give me the black tar” she said.

“Fine suit yerself” he said swiveling around and opening the bottom drawer.

Maggie kept pressing the button hoping to get to a reality where she never went to Owen’s office in the first place but gave up after a while. She fed the doodad the coordinates to her sister’s apartment four years ago. She saw Haley sitting at the kitchen table hands locked on the tabletop as though in prayer across from her ghost self who sat facing away.

“I’m sorry Maggie I’m just… I’m sorry…” Haley said.

“I know…” Maggie said

“It’s just… I can’t… I can’t anymore…”

“I know…”

“… And I’m pregnant now…”

Maggie started crying “I know… I would like to meet him though” Maggie said. Her sister started crying too. She reached for Maggie’s hand.

“Just take rehab and you can stay how hard is that?” Haley said.

“I can’t, Haley… I’ve tried…”

“Try again… You’re not a junkie, Maggie, you know that…”

“Just give me a few months… I’ll find somewhere else to stay, I just need a few months…”

“You’ve said that before”
“Just a few months, so I can get my shit in order.”

“No, Maggie… I’m either driving you to the rehab center right now or you’re on your own.”

Maggie looked her sister in the eye, “Haley, you’re being mean.”

Haley wiped her tears, looked away. “I’m sorry Maggie… You should pack up, now.”

Maggie pushed the off button and put the doodad down. She grabbed her kit from her purse, cooked everything she had, and loaded the spike. Four grams or more in there could take out someone that was already dead. She squeezed the plunger and saw the stream draw a trail between her legs leading to the entrance to the bandstand. She strapped her arm up and licked the needle. A car drove by blaring Bill Withers’ “Lovely Day” and she didn’t find it funny.

She closed her eyes and sighed. Can’t hurt to take one more look. She put the spike between her teeth, grabbed the doodad and set the date and time to here now. She saw her ghost self shooting up her arm, closing her eyes, and dying, thick spit trickling down her hung head.

As the sun started to rise and wash away the fog, she noticed the ghosts started growing fainter. The lights on the doodad started to flutter. It was running out of juice. She kept flicking through but it was all the same thing: the same corpse lying in shit and piss jumping from place to place in the bandstand.

Then she pressed the button and saw nothing. She looked around, no corpse. Was there something climbing the steps to the bandstand? She squinted. Sure enough, there it was: a fuzzy thing she had to go up to it to see it clearly. It was another ghost self but difficult to recognize. This one was wearing clean jeans and a tank top and her skin, though a little blistered and scabbed, looked a little plumper like it was actually covering a living thing. The ghost folded her arms on the railing of the bandstand and watched the magenta sunrise splash over the park. She then took a medicine bottle from her pocket. Maggie could barely read the label but eventually she saw it said “Retrovir.” The ghost swallowed a tablet from it. She looked tired but peaceful. Maggie’d seen many sunrises from long nights out but couldn’t remember looking at one that way before. Like it wasn’t a burden.

Maggie looked at the device. The lights were barely flashing now. The ghost was blending into the air. If she pressed the other button she would take the ghost’s place in that reality. But then again she and this other Maggie were still standing here. Whatever was in that other one that allowed her to pick life over death was also inside herself.

Maggie turned away from the ghost, spat out the syringe and stepped on it. She put the doodad on the railing and climbed out of the bandstand. The tint of magenta was now turning amber like a honey jar. The grass in the park was dewy like hair after a shower. She heard the first buses, cars and birds. The city was yawning.

The bug might kill me she thought but it won’t beat me.
